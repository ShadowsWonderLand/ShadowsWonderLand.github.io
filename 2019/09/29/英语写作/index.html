Euphemism (委婉语)

an inoffensive expression that is substituted for one that is considered offensive

  • e.g.: You are well fed. (You are fat)
  • ​ We have to let you go (You are fired.)

Hyperbole (夸张)

The use of exaggerated terms for the purpose of emphasis or heightened effect.

  • e.g.: When she heard the bad news, a river of tears poured out.

Irony (讽刺)

The use of words to convey the opposite of their literal meaning.

  • e.g.: It would be a fine thing indeed not knowing what time it was in the morning.
  • This hard-working boy seldom reads more than an hour per week.

Metaphor

An implied comparison between two unlike things that actually have sth important in common.

​ e.g.: The sofa is fertile soil for a couch potato.

Simile

A stated comparison (usually formed with “like” or “as”) between two fundamentally dissimilar things that have qualities in common.

  • e.g.: She looks like a flower but stings like a bee.

metonymy

A figure of speech in which one word or phrase is substituted for another with which it is closely related. (describing sth indirectly by referring to things around it)

  • e.g.: He writes a fine hand. (he has good hand writing.)

Synecdoche

A figure of speech in which a part is used for the whole (as hand for sailor), the whole for a part (as the law for police officer), the specific for the general (as cutthroat for assassin), the general for the specific (as thief for pickpocket), or the material for the thing from which it is made (as steel for sword ).

  • e.g.: ABCs (alphabet)
  • e.g.: England won the World Cup in 1966.

(Football team of England)

Personification

A figure of speech in which an inanimate object is endowed with human qualities or abilities.

  • e.g.: The carved pumpkin smiled at me.

It ia usually neutral.

Prejudice

  • a partiality that prevents objective consideration of an issue or situation.It ususally have negative meaning.

Context

  • discourse that surrounds a language unit and helps to determine its interpretation.

Objective

  • based on facts, or making a decision that is based on facts rather than on your feelings or beliefs

Sujective

  • a statement, report, attitude etc is influenced by personal opinion and can therefore be unfair.

Thesis Statement

  • A thesis statement usually appears near the end of the introductory paragraph of a paper, and it offers a concise summary of the main point or claim of the essay, research paper, etc.

Point of view

  • a particular way of thinking about or judging a situation 视角，角度

Emotive

  • characterized by emotion

Factual

  • based on facts or relating to facts

Critical thinking

Conclusion

  • something you decide after considering all the information you have

Fiction

  • a literary work based on the imagination and not necessarily on fact

Critical Thinking – Possible Questions

  • What is critical thinking?

    An effective, logical approach to academic work. It encourages students to reflect on what they learn.

critical thinking is defined as:

  • “the process of actively and skillfully conceptualizing, applying, analyzing, and evaluating information to reach an answer or conclusion
  • Name four skills you associate with critical thinking.

analysis

questioning

indepentent learning

​ applying

  • How does critical thinking improve a person’s quality of reading (or writing, or both)?

Encourages analysis and questioning of texts.

Helps us detect bias, prejudice, inaccuracies, limitations or contradictions.

Facilitates independent learning.

  • How can critical thinking help students?

    Helps students distinguish between what is essential, what is possibly useful and what is probably irrelevant.

The wording of the question and number of marks should act as a guide as to how much detail to include.

Briefly Describe Three Elements of Critical Thinking

  • It encourages individuals to analyse and ask questions rather than just accept things at face value.

  • It helps individuals to identify bias or prejudice and to formulate arguments based on evidence.

  • It helps individuals to understand the importance of historical and social context to topics studied.

There are others. As this says ‘describe’ some detail is needed, but a 12 mark question requires much more.

Features of Academic Writing

  • Structure – introduction, body paragraphs (or main body), and conclusion. There should be a logical sequence.

  • Purpose – can include inform, explain, compare and contrast, justify, analyse etc.

  • Style – should be impersonal, objective, well-reasoned, and based upon carefully selected evidence.

  • It includes careful analysis and critical thinking.

Context

  • Basically the what, where? when? who? how? and why? that readers need to know.

  • Context helps the reader understand cultural, social, economic, political, philosophical and religious ideas.

  • Some of the main things that affect context are time period, gender, race, religion, social, class, author’s purpose and bias etc.

  • Context varies according to situation/type of writing etc.

Bias

  • A one-sided view or account of a situation.a partiality that prevents objective consideration of an issue or situation.

  • Seeks to pass opinion of as fact; often unsupported or based on flawed/twisted logic.

  • It may reveal the author’s preferences or prejudices.

  • It may be deliberate or accidental, subtle or blatant, conscious or sub-conscious.

  • Politicians use biased language to attack their rivals.

  • Other examples?

​ trademen use bias language to sell goods.

Bias

  • You may need to define bias and/or identify different types of bias.

  • You may encounter bias in a reading text.

  • You may have to change a sentence to make it biased, or make a biased sentence more objective.

  • You may have to write in a biased style.

Perspective

  • The writer’s perspective can be an important element of context, as can the reader’s.

  • An individual’s perspective is shaped by the time period; their social class; their age and gender; family influence; political, religious, social and cultural issues etc.

  • A person’s perspective might reveal their bias.

  • A person’s perspective often helps us understand motive.

Plagiarism

Possible Questions

  • What is it?

Plagiarism means copying work from another source such as textbook or an article online and presented as being your own work. It may be a few lines, a couple of paragraphs or a whole section.

  • Why is it considered a problem in academic writing?

It is a form of intellectual property theft in academic writing. It is cheating.

  • Why is it so widespread?

Because Students do not know enough about the importance of Academic writing and online resources are easily gained.

  • How can plagiarism be avoided? (eg Explain two ways …..)

    Use quotation marks whenever quoting an exact phrase, sentence or short paragraph

    Make sure you acknowledged and cited all sources of borrowed ideas and materials.

  • Is plagiarism always a bad thing?

No,sometimes make reference to others‘ opinion can widen our horizon，but can’t totally use others’ opinion and represent it as our own.

Part Three

  • Read the text and the question carefully.

  • Use quotations to support the arguments you put forward and make sure you link them to the question.

  • Include critical thinking – do not just accept what you are told at face value. Is it honest, accurate, reliable, logical etc?
  • Make sure you write enough.
  • Check your writing at the end to avoid silly mistakes.

Part Four

  • Here you will have to argue something. It will not be something you have looked at in class.

  • Try to put forward views in support of and against the issue or quotation, but come down on one side.

  • Include as many references as you can to recent events, history, literature, personal experience etc as you can to support your arguments.

  • Use appropriate language and structure.

  • Follow instructions carefully.
